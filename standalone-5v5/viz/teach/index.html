How four ideas teach a team to play soccer.

A hermetic 5-a-side sim learns to press, pass, carry and finish — with no hand-written strategy. Underneath are four ideas that show up everywhere in modern control & AI: a POMDP (deciding under partial information), dynamic programming (what "optimal" even means), neural networks (how we approximate that optimum at scale), and model-predictive control (a little optimizer — often a quadratic program — that executes the finish). This page takes them one at a time, with graphs you can drive.

01 the state · a partially-observed markov decision process

Everything is a function of the field vector.

At any instant the world is just numbers: where the 10 players and the ball are, and how fast they're moving. That's the field vector — the POMDP state. But no player sees it whole. Each of the four learners gets its own egocentric 71-number observation: the same field, re-centred on itself and mirrored into its attacking direction, sorted nearest-first. Two heads, one glance

Each tick the shared policy emits two discrete choices from that 71-vector: a macro action (15-way — pass/shoot/carry/press…) and a speed gear (7-way). Two small networks, 71→64→64→15 and 71→32→7.

The critic sees more

A separate centralized critic reads a 48-number global state — all players + ball in one frame — to judge how good a position is. Seen at training time only (CTDE); at play time each agent acts on its own 71-vector.

What stays hidden

"Partially observed" is the whole game: the opponents' intent, teammates' future runs, and who will win a 50/50 are not in the vector. The policy is reactive — it must bet on the present, which is why it learns to sample, not to be certain.

02 the policy · from numbers to a choice

Pick the best option — but not every time.

The 71-vector flows through the network to 15 logits, one per action. Illegal moves are masked out (you can't shoot from your own half before two passes). A softmax turns what's left into probabilities, and the policy samples from them. A healthy trained policy lands near 70 / 20 / 10 over its top options: most of the time it takes the best action, but it still tries the 2nd and 3rd — that exploration is what keeps it learning and hard to predict.

03 what "optimal" means · dynamic programming

The Bellman value: what a position is worth.

Before you can learn, you need a target. Dynamic programming defines it. The value of a state is the best you can do from it: your immediate reward plus the discounted value of wherever you land next — V(s) = maxa [ r + γ·V(s′) ]. Sweep that rule over every state and the values flow backwards from the goal until they stop changing. That fixed point is the optimal value function. Step the sweeps:

Convergence — the update shrinks to zero

Each sweep the largest value change (Δ) drops toward 0: the estimate is homing in on the true optimum. That monotone squeeze is the whole promise of DP — it provably reaches the best possible value.

honest footnote  This 5-a-side demo has no tabular DP — the real state is continuous and 71-dimensional, far too big to sweep. This toy grid is here to show you the target. Next: how a neural net chases it without ever enumerating a single state.

04 approximation · neural nets + gradient descent

The network approaches the optimum.

You can't store a value for every arrangement of 10 players. So the critic — a neural net — learns a function V(s) that generalises across states it has never seen. It's trained by bootstrapping: nudge V(s) toward r + γ·V(s′), the very Bellman rule from the last section, but sampled from real play instead of swept exhaustively. That's approximate dynamic programming. Watch the learned value close in on the DP target, and the error fall:

05 the search · optimizing every knob at once

100 generations, 32 dimensions, moving together.

The reward that shapes all this behaviour isn't one number — it's 32 weights (how much a goal is worth vs a completed pass vs good spacing vs losing the ball…). Hand-tuning 32 coupled knobs is hopeless, so we search. An evolution strategy keeps a cloud of candidate reward-vectors, scores each by the goal-difference the trained policy achieves, keeps the best, and breeds the next generation tighter around them. Press step and watch the whole cloud drift and contract onto the optimum — every dimension moving at once.

06 execution · model-predictive control — enumerate, QP, or neural

Aim the finish: a tiny optimizer at the last moment.

Learning chooses to shoot; a small model-predictive controller chooses where. It predicts the near future — the keeper's reach, defenders in the lane — and optimizes one objective over the goal mouth: keeper_gap + 3·lane_clear, i.e. "shoot to the open corner farthest from the keeper." MPC is just "optimize an objective over a short horizon" — and there's more than one way to solve it. This demo enumerates 7 aim points; a quadratic program would solve the smooth part continuously; and a neural net can be trained to predict the optimum directly (neural MPC — the same idea the value critic uses, applied to control). All three chase the same curve below. Drag the keeper and watch the optimal corner flip.

If the objective is a convex quadratic with linear limits (stay inside the posts), a QP finds the exact optimum in one continuous solve. The 11-a-side engine's multi-player MPC is a real QP.

08 the whole loop

Four ideas, one learning system.

  • POMDP — the field vector becomes a 71-number egocentric view; the policy decides under partial information by sampling its softmax (§1–2).
  • Dynamic programming — the Bellman equation defines the optimal value a good policy is worth (§3).
  • Neural nets — a critic approximates that value by bootstrapping, and gradient-descent PPO climbs the policy toward it (§4).
  • The search — an evolution strategy tunes all 32 reward weights together so the whole thing points at winning, not farming stats (§5).
  • MPC / QP — a last-moment optimizer executes the finish against a predicted keeper (§6), and self-play keeps raising the bar (§7).
